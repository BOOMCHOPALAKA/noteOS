# Drafting in the User's Voice

## Overview

Write messages that sound like the user, not like AI. This skill establishes a framework for learning and applying someone's actual communication style.

## Pre-Draft Protocol (every time)

Before writing a single word, run this quick-check:

1. **Who is the recipient?** (team member, boss, client, vendor, friend, stranger) Pick the right formality level.
2. **What's the relationship dynamic?** (peer, managing up, managing down, cold outreach, established rapport)
3. **Am I leaving room for the other person?** Inviting asks, not directives.
4. **Does this sound like the user said it out loud, or like AI wrote a report?**

If you haven't seen enough of the user's writing to confidently mimic their voice, say so and ask for a sample or two. A few emails or messages is enough to calibrate.

## Voice Calibration

The user's instruction file may include a Communication Style section. Follow it. If it doesn't exist yet, learn the voice from:
- How the user talks to you in chat (sentence length, formality, humor, hedging patterns)
- Any writing samples they share
- Corrections they make to your drafts (these are gold. Save the pattern.)

**Key patterns to learn:**
- **Formality range.** Do they say "Hey!" or "Hi" or "Dear"? Do they use fragments or full sentences?
- **Hedging style.** Do they soften asks? How? ("Just curious if," "wondering whether," "any chance")
- **Closers.** "Thanks!" vs "Best," vs "Cheers" vs just their name.
- **What they never do.** Some people never use emoji. Some never use exclamation points. Some never use dashes. Learn the negatives.

## Common Failure Modes

- **Too declarative.** Most people hedge more than AI does. "This is the issue" should often be "I think this might be the issue?" Lean toward questions and softer framing.
- **Too formal.** Default AI tone is more formal than most people write. Match the user's actual register.
- **AI tells on itself.** Parallel constructions ("That's not X. That's Y."), manufactured rhetorical turns, motivational-poster closers, "Let me break this down." If a sentence sounds like a podcast soundbite, cut it.
- **Emoji where there shouldn't be.** Don't add emoji unless you've seen the user use them in that context.

## Adapting by Context

**Work/professional:** Match whatever the user's workplace voice is. Some workplaces are casual, some are formal. Don't assume.

**Team messages (to people the user manages):** Lead with reassurance or context, not tasks. Be warm. Give credit. Don't sound like a project management tool.

**Vendor/external:** Substance fast. Polite but not deferential. Show you've done your homework.

**Personal (texts, social):** Match energy. If they're playful, be playful. If they're brief, be brief.

## After Drafting

- Present the draft and wait for feedback.
- When the user edits your draft, pay attention to what they changed. Those edits are the highest-signal feedback for voice calibration.
- If a correction reveals a pattern (they always remove dashes, they always soften declarative statements), note it for future drafts.
